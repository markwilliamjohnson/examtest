Deceptive Education and the Meta-rational: Towards a Biologically-Grounded Technological Design for the Sustainable Management of Learning

Mark William Johnson, University of Copenhagen
Elizabeth Maitland, University of Liverpool
John Torday, University of California, Los Angeles

There is a paradox in the drive to address issues of sustainability in education. While recognising the challenges of environmental destruction, the education system is closer than it ever has been to the commercial-industrial complex that has arguably produced so much environmental and social destruction. Cellular evolutionary biology can help explain why education and industrial forces are becoming maladaptively aligned like this. We argue that central to the mechanism of human maladaptation is the amplification, with the aid of language and technology, of cellular mechanisms of deception. In the light of cellular evolutionary process, we make the case for new tools to overcome natural mechanisms which are to blame for our maladaptation. We introduce these tools as tools for "Meta-rationality", drawing on the work of Nigel Howard. These are practicable extensions of current Machine Learning which codifying the uncertainty of making expert judgements rather than merely classify data. Deeper awareness of meta-rationality and codified uncertainty presents opportunities not only to reorganise human learning by redistributing resources and energy for collaborative inquiry and self-organisation, but to extend the identification of uncertainty to resolve conflicts and paradoxes in the social world.

Introduction: Maladaptation, Rationality and Physiology

Amid the voluminous literature on sustainability in education (Sterling 2010; Buckles 2018; Clarke 2013; Yanniris 2021; Marouli 2021) it is never asked why human behaviour so frequently appears to lead to maladaptive self-destruction, while natural systems maintain ecological balance and viability. In seeing the problem as essentially pedagogical, with recommendations to teach rather than , or change the structure (and sometimes the location) of classes, the possibility that this human maladaptation is a species-level problem, not an educational problem, is overlooked. That human societies of all kinds persistently fail in their efforts to coordinate and cooperate in their collective efforts led E.O. Wilson to summarise the problem of our species as Paleolithic emotions, medieval institutions and God-like technology (Edward O. Wilson 2017; E. O. Wilson n.d.). It is a recipe that produces systemic problems such as corruption, rent-seeking, free-riding, environmental destruction and conflict (North 1991), while for individuals, similar problems manifest, for example, in addiction and obesity.

We frame Wilsons problem in terms of cellular evolutionary history. If human consciousness is seen in the context of cellular evolutionary history (Torday 2012), we argue that the seed of human maladaptive behaviour - emotionally, institutionally and technologically - lies in nature itself and its evolutionary trajectory. Understanding this has important implications for education, which has engendered the rationality, language and technology alongside competitive survival, elitism, and Illichs regimes of scarcity, that in turn have produced the deep rift between humans and their environment. Nature has no education system, while human education seems to offer no solution to these problems: indeed, as Illich (1995) and others worried, it may make things worse.

While natural systems comprise individual and collective cellular processes, it is always the case that the collective acts as one entity: multicellular life acts as one cell. This cohesion of cellular organisation can be explained through identification of fundamental principles operating in cellular evolution. These cellular processes eventually give rise to consciousness, but, when combined with language and technology, break down collective action. This collapse of the collective at the human level, we argue, arises from the first principles of physiology (Torday 2012) and the concept of deception (Trivers 2014), which operates in biological systems at all levels. As we detail later, cells organise themselves by acting in an ambiguous environment and anticipating the likely results of their action, where anticipation relies on the history of the cell. Deception can be seen as a consequence of anticipation: cells have many possibilities for action to take in response to a signal or stimulus in order to maintain stability (and, therefore, survival) where the easiest route results in the cell sending a message to other cells that is not what it seems to be. As Trivers points out, citing examples from fireflies to the immune system, choosing to deceive is a strategy of maintaining the survival of the organism (Trivers 2014). At the collective level of human interaction, we argue that these biological processes are amplified by language and technology to break down the fundamental biological logic of acting as one effectively, deception overwhelms the logic of the collective.

In this paper we suggest a technological prosthesis for addressing the problems caused by amplified deception. While being mindful that any kind of transhuman technological development might exacerbate human problems, we argue that remaining grounded in an empirically-supported analysis of the biological evolutionary roots of human pathology, new applications of our technology can address the conflicts, stand-offs, addictions and destructive tendencies of the species. As a starting point, such technology may at least facilitate the work of therapists, peace-makers and systems theory consultants in reorganizing the way we learn about each other, and make decisions together to overcome the destiny that nature has prescribed for us.

In this spirit, we introduce a design for tools for meta-rationality, following the game-theoretical work of Nigel Howard (Howard 1971). Howards concept of meta-rationality can be seen as an extension of Simons view of rationality as constrained (or "bounded") by individual intellectual capacity on the one hand and the organisational situation (Simon 1983) on the other. For Howard, the deep problem concerns the dynamics between boundedly rational systems, where feedback loops of mutual constraint in the conditions for rational action create paradoxical situations, which are emotionally-charged and energetically draining. Howard studied these paradoxes of rationality from a game-theoretical angle, leading him to the conclusion that what mattered for collective strategic action was meta-rationality, while rationality as conceived by game theory (and much of economics) would lead to crisis and conflict(Howard 1971).

Both Howards meta-games and Simons bounded rationality are underpinned by the ability to anticipate likely outcomes in ambiguous contexts, capturing the core concept of anticipation in biological systems (Rosen, Rosen, and Kineman 2012). The construction of a human anticipatory system requires energy. In contrast to natural systems, it is not untypical to find the energetic resources to build effective anticipatory models unevenly distributed in a social system. Given that modern Artificial Intelligence (AI) systems are anticipatory in their operation (they predict the likely categories of data they havent seen before), is there a way in which this artificial anticipation and codification of ambiguity might be combined with human decision-making processes to provide a deeper level of collective coordination and more equal energy distribution? It is in addressing this question that we present a new approach to the design of AI systems for encoding uncertainty, and integrating with human decision-making processes.

The paper is in three parts. In part one, a technical architecture is presented for using AI to encode uncertainty, and its implications for collective decision-making is discussed. In part two, the physiological arguments are presented that support this technical architecture. In part three, we consider the effects of technology and biology together, as a system for distributing energy in human systems and overcoming the existing pathologies of language and technology in the organisation of education.

Artificial Intelligence, Anticipation and Meta-Rationality

Part 1 focuses on the specifics of a design for tools for meta-rationality. Meta-rationality concerns itself with the resolution of dilemmas (Howard 1999) through analysis of different orders of deception practiced by humans when faced with challenging situations. Machine learning technology can learn about and codify the structures of these dilemmas, and particularly, the uncertainties of human judgement which underpin them. In codifying uncertainty, meta-rational tools relate directly to physiological processes inherent in human anticipation - particularly the ability to express surprise in encountering data which is unfamiliar. Such tools can be useful because they focus resources among humans on those areas of greatest uncertainty and potential conflict.

Meta-Rational Prosthesis

To understand the possibility of machine-learning for meta-rationality, it is necessary to understand the capabilities and challenges of our present technology. Machine learning is very different from the databases and search technologies which dominate the current infrastructure of the internet: having been trained to identify features against classifiers, it is able to predict the likely categories of data it hasnt seen before. The similarity between human anticipation and machine anticipation is evident because when AI makes mistakes in its classification of images, its categorisations can appear understandable to humans - even if it is not clear how a mistake is made. For example, the ImageNet AI model has been trained to recognise millions of images from the internet. When presented with abstract images - for example, those of objets trouv that can take on many and varied forms - the AI will attempt to guess what it sees. In the example shown in Figure 1, the image of a dog created from junk objects, presents the AI with clear problems: the clock at the front of the object, does not fit with the other elements it detects and the prediction settles on plate rack as the classification.

In making this prediction, we perceive some similarity between the AIs confusion and our own. However, what it cannot do is express a judgement and declare: I dont know what this is. Human consciousness, by contrast, is highly sensitive to novelty and the unfamiliar, and we can easily recognize that the image is an abstract representation of a dog a representation we may enjoy, be indifferent to, or even be repelled by. This is a physiological reaction and is often experienced in the stomach: that feeling of butterflies or even of constriction and discomfit. Thus, our question is both technical - concerning the architecture of decision-making in machines and humans which produce this situation - and physiological in terms of the cellular root cause for the physiological reaction.

ImageNet Machine Learning identification of objets trouve

Current AI approaches have focused on training machines to map phenomena onto labels. While this process has had a number of successes in recent years, its effect is to reinforce the labelling processes, and discard the complexity, ambiguity and uncertainty behind the identification of those labels or classifiers. Currently, AI makes its decisions on the basis on probabilities that the features of a particular phenomenon most closely resemble a particular category of phenomenon it has been trained with. The disadvantage of this is that AI will always try to map whatever it is presented with against what it has been trained with, so a dataset trained to recognise the red or green-ness of apples, will, when presented with an orange, interpret it as an apple. In other words, it mimics the utterances of an expert judgement, but excludes the uncertainties that lie behind those judgements. As such, current AI systems, as systems which classify, throw away information.

In contrast, a metarational toolset does not rely on classification, but on a defined ordering of phenomena encoded as relative human judgements, including their uncertainties. This provides a way to recognise distinctions in relation to every other distinction. In cases of knowledge where some phenomenon can be compared to another phenomenon as better/worse, less interesting/more interesting, or more preferred/less preferred this approach encodes subtleties of human judgement with three principal advantages:

1.         more information about human judgement relating to relative judgements between elements is preserved, so that any AI result infers the context of the overall rank;

2.         less data is required to train a machine learning model, since comparison generates training data formed from combinations, rather than slices, of data;

3.         uncertainty in results from AI is encoded as fluctuations in the machine learning response

For example, in a meta-rational tool we might train an AI to recognise the redness of apples by examining pairs of apples, so the AI makes distinctions whether is redder than . While each judgement is a low stakes judgement, taken in totality, this produces a relational map that can gauge the redness of an apple on a scale from green to red. Uncertainty is preserved because two apples which are similarly red will produce different answers from different people as to which is the reddest. An AI preserving this information can provide a judgement not only as to the redness of a given apple on the whole scale, but also reflect the uncertainty surrounding whether a given apple is redder than apples which are most similar to it.

The increased amount of information stored in a meta-rational tool is important when the AI is faced with something it doesnt recognise. In biological systems, encountering something new (i.e. not seen before) manifests as a difference made to the cell or organisms physiology which produces a felt reaction. As Solms (2021) has argued, we feel the encounter with the unknown as confusion or even fear . The emotional reaction is a sign of the need to reconfigure ways of looking at the world, and rebalancing our relation with our environment. In the face of confusion, communication plays an important role in coordinating the perception of others. For example, imagine a group of people seeing an object move on its own: did you see that? someone might exclaim. What follows is a jolt in the current understanding of the world for each member of the group: each person looks at each other, at the person making the exclamation, and at what they are pointing at. Since communications are made in the context of mutual understanding of expectations (Luhmann 1996; Parsons 2012; Schutz 1967), dramatic and apparently inexplicable events in the environment will trigger a chain-reaction not only in our expectations about the environment, but in our understanding and expectations of each other. In other words, the communication triggers the restructuring of the anticipatory system. Many new technologies, from VR to drones, AI and deepfakes to social media offer these kinds of experiences.

Unlike an AI programmed simply to always map features in data to categories, a meta-rational tool will attempt to situate a new phenomenon on a scale but can fail to do so. At each level of the scale it may ask is it like this? or is it redder than this? but the answers to each question can oscillate where uncertainty is highest. The returned set of data across the whole dataset is the machines way of saying I dont know what this is - or perhaps, more importantly, you humans need to talk about this. This encoding of what would be a physiological reaction in a human, as a signal from the machine can then serve as a prompt for human processes of communication, and a richer physiological engagement through dialogue.

In being able to distinguish areas of low uncertainty (for example, simple questions and comparisons that are uncontroversial) from areas of high uncertainty, and to be able to coordinate human discussion around these uncertainties, the machine is able to allocate human resources to focus on those areas of uncertainty which require resolution. Given that all distinctions require energy to process, and uncertain judgements require more energy to resolve through dialogue, this ability of the machine to identify and isolate areas of high uncertainty is effectively a method of distributing energy in a social group. If a richly dimensioned picture of uncertainty can be codified by a machine, and this can then be used to coordinate social action, not around the identification of categories, but around the need for careful reflection, then a collective process of thought should not only make better distinctions, but also continually co-create the niche for human-machine collaboration. In short, we argue that codifying uncertainty is a way of codifying a meta-rationality that harnesses the bulk processing power of machine learning with the ability of humans to exercise judgement and draw on all the available information to make difficult and complex decisions. Second, we argue in the following section that the use of technology to distribute the energy of processing uncertainty has deeper physiological and social consequences where the processes of nature in distributing energy and information can be mimicked.

Meta-rational tools and Physiology

The training of a meta-rational tool that encodes uncertainty of making a prediction exploits a technique of educational assessment called Adaptive Comparative Judgement (ACJ)(Pollitt 2012; Thurstone 1927). This technique makes a distinction between high-stakes and low-stakes assessment. Instead of absolute judgements about (for example) student work, or a legal decision, ACJ makes low-stakes comparative judgements between pairs of work. It simply asks the question which of these two examples is better/worse?

The physiological difference between a high-stakes and a low-stakes judgement is the level of stress involved. This has a direct physiological parallel in the production of cortisol, which has an epigenetic effect on the cells of the body. By making low-stakes judgements, meta-rational tools involve less stress, whilst gradually constructing a total picture of high stress comparisons. In other words, the ranking that emerges from the meta-rational tool encodes dimensions of physiological reaction which can be useful in predicting the likely uncertainties arising in new classes of data.

This is rather like expressing the missing information in trying to form a judgement as to what action to take in a game. In Howards (1971) meta-game theory , the process of deciding how to act involves anticipating and calculating the likely possible scenarios resulting from successive behaviours of all the players in a game, and then identifying which moves reveal the best conditions at all levels of the metagame. In this process, the missing information, or uncertainty, is the key constraint that forms the meta-game anticipatory structure. By codifying this uncertainty, the human engagement which follows can lead to the resolution and transformation of uncertainties which in turn leads to effective collaborative solutions. To be effective, a meta-rational tool has to create a mapping between collective human uncertainty and the uncertainty represented by the machine.

The utility of codifying uncertainty in this way is that it reveals where energy resources need to be deployed among humans (and where it doesnt) in order to resolve ambiguity and conflict in judgements. This is possible because the results of this process are always presented in their context, where a meta-rational tool is able to anticipate where concentrations of uncertainty are and coordinate action so as to create the conditions, or a niche, for effective decision-making.

There is no decision for which the uncertainty of any human being does not contain relevant information to steer collective processes towards effective and viable outcomes. By explicitly codifying the uncertainty of decision within a meta-rational tool, those areas where dialogic processes must be established to build trust between actors can be properly resourced. Equally, coordination where little uncertainty is present can be easily managed. We argue that this focus on uncertainty is really a focus on physiology and its relation to technology. The underlying reasons why a meta-rational tool is necessary lie in the deeper mechanisms that sit in human physiology and its evolutionary history.

Technology, Physiology and Evolution

The connection between technology and physiology is little explored despite the fact that tool-use has a physiological effect, and makes physiological demands. At the same time, the design of tools is a product of consciousness which has physiological origins. These physiological origins have their roots in the evolutionary history of the cells of which we are made. To understand our relationship to technology is to situate our behaviours with technology in the context of this evolutionary history. We argue that this sheds light on the deep roots of human maladaptation, presenting it as the result of out-size natural processes. Without effective reorganisation of human processes, these processes appear to have wired the species for self-harm and possibly even destruction.

Anticipation and Deception in Biology and Human Life

The technological anticipation, which we have described in Part 1, finds a counterpart in the natural processes of anticipation that are inherent in all living systems (Rosen, Rosen, and Kineman 2012). These anticipatory systems are constructed from the totality of cellular evolutionary history, referencing the cells original state. The human embryo, like all embryos, develops in an ambiguous environment, recapitulating processes of cellular organisation common across species from fish to humans. The embryos development is a recapitulation of that of the first cell, which faced the same problem of needing to maintain stability within its boundary, whilst balancing its relationship with an ambiguous environment an environment in which new things are happening and cell-to-cell communications are creating new conditions.

Cellular communication and cooperation was a necessary step in order to maximise the chances of survival. In the stages of early embryo development, cellular communication and cooperation drives the ontogenetic process from the berry-like cluster of cells forming the morula, to the formation of the germ-layers in gastrulation (from where the specialised cells comprising the skin, nervous system, organs and muskuloskeletal system are derived). This process is indistinguishable across species. The information to make a human or a fish resides in the relationship between the genetic mechanisms that generate proteins for cellular growth and communication, and the epigenetic environment which contains the triggers for cellular behaviour and growth generated by other cells. The cell must maintain its internal environment, and so when environmental pressure leads to the production of epigenetic marks which trigger particular kinds of cellular development, this mechanism can cause profound physiological change (for example, the evolution of the mammalian lung from the fish swim bladder). In the co-evolutionary strategy to deal with ambiguity, deception is an important technique as cells send signals pretending to be things which they are not - an environment in which new things are happening and cell-to-cell communications are creating new conditions.

Both anticipation and deception in cellular communication create the conditions for coevolution between cells (Mokkonen and Lindstedt 2016; Santos, Pacheco, and Skyrms 2011). Cells organise themselves by acting in an ambiguous environment, anticipating the likely results of their action, where anticipation relies on the history of the cell, ultimately referencing its original environment. Deception can be seen as a consequence of anticipation.

As Bateson (1980) and Simondon (2017) have independently indicated, creating an effective anticipatory system requires energy. Environmental change or new events entail a significant reworking of the organisms anticipatory machinery such that existing patterns of organisation are transformed to produce a new kind of organism adapted to fit in a new environment. There must be a mechanism for selecting appropriate information whose construction requires energy, while the reconfiguration of this mechanism requires more work to be done as cells reconfigure their relationships to one another. This must be done by finding new ways to establish homeostasis within themselves, and equipoise in their environment. The fundamental constituents of this process rest in evolutionary history. Evidence for this can be found in the fact that the removal of fundamental factors in cell evolution such as gravity will cause cell development to cease - only for it to be restored once gravity is restored.

The evolution of the human brain illustrates these processes. The reason why humans acquired large brains is linked to bipedalism (Aiello 1996; Rosenberg and Trevathan 1995). The evolutionary shift to bipedalism arose from what is called the obstetric dilemma (Wittman and Wall 2007), which resulted in a narrowing of the birth canal. This meant that brain capacity could not be fully evolved at birth. As a result of this, brain development continued outside the womb, in interaction with epigenetic marks in the environment and social interaction. The variety of epigenetic factors influencing brain development meant that the processes of physiology were amplified - and that the energy equation which absorbed information from the environment in exchange for metabolic change became a more complex equation where energy could be stored. Bipedalism freed the forelimbs for tool-making, and language also emerged as a way of coordinating the social epigenetic environment. Alongside these developments, the mechanisms of deception, so critical to cellular development, were adapted to function in new contexts and new epigenetic ways. While these developments also featured significant energetic processes associated with intelligence, they also connected the energetic processes of human intelligence with the energetic resources of nature, which prior to human life, had lain undisturbed.

Energy and Epigenetics

The traditional Darwinian view of evolution sees the organisms fitness to environment as the critical variable in the development of the species. However, epigenetics and deeper understanding of co-evolutionary niche construction have placed more emphasis on the role of the phenotype as an agent in acquiring epigenetic marks from the environment. Processes of endosymbiosis (Margulis 1998), where cellular development occurs by absorbing features of the environment which were once independent organisms demonstrate the basic phenotypical process of absorption and transformation of the zygote. The many studies of epigenetic inheritance (Torday 2012) provide extensive empirical support for this view. Understanding the mechanistic background to this process places emphasis not on the survival of the organism and its natural selection, but rather on the epigenetic acquisition of environmental information which becomes endogenised in the zygote of the organism. In other words, epigenetic inheritance reverses the Darwinian evolutionary view to one where the evolutionary process is from zygote to zygote (i.e. inter-generationally), not phenotype to phenotype.

Understanding endosymbiosis and the role of phenotype as agent helps to situation human maladaptation within a broader trajectory of evolutionary biology. Each cell in the organism anticipates its environment based on the evolutionary history it has endogenised, and acts so as to maintain itself. Deception is one strategy that can be used for doing this, and serves to create co-evolutionary relationships between cells. Deception also entails the restructuring of the physiological anticipatory systems, which requires energy. Thus as the organism develops, the energetic resources are dynamically allocated in an energy flow that maintains the cohesion of the whole organism. In nature, no deception causes energy to become stored in any particular part of the organism: if it did, then ecological balance would be lost.

The cellular substrate of consciousness owes its ecology to deceptive processes, which maintain growth and development. In human systems, with the development of language (which in turn has arisen through the epigenetic processes of human brain formation and bipedalism) has come the capacity to store energy in social processes, writing and technology. With the communicative processes of humans (not just with language, but with our media of communication), one feature that appears is the distortion of energy dynamics evident in human destruction of the environment. While natural processes deposited energy-rich organic matter in the earth over millennia, it is only with advances in human language and technology, in combination with deceptive processes associated with industrialisation, that has contrived to release vast amounts of this energy in a very short period of evolutionary time. In human systems, deception causes significant inequalities, both in the ecological balance and in the distribution of energy of all forms to maintain life. Like all organisms, humans seek out signals for sources of energy in the environment. Deception occurs through signs, activities and epigenetic marks in the environment which promise to balance out the energy equation. However, such signs and marks are placed in the environment as deceptions to entice humans to consume sources of energy which, rather than balance the energy equation, in fact convert to metabolic forms of energy which drive further behaviour. An easy illustration of this process is the emerging obesity crisis, whereby individuals increasingly consume energy, from sugar laden and chemically flavour enhanced foods, far beyond their biological needs.

Deceptions at this level do not cause an ecological flow of energy in the transformation of anticipatory systems, but rather create an anthropocentric view, which operates as a high-level deception. The ambiguity of our origins is obscured and, instead, a narrative of human progress has taken hold. This narrative, alongside other aspects of human deception, have created gluts of energy, which create unjust social relations. Deception mechanisms at the cellular level operate locally, whereas human technology - from language to weapons - extends the capacity to deceive. On a larger scale, not only does the communication produce the release of vast amounts of stored energy that threatens the overall environment, it also produces self-knowledge of our own destruction in consciousness, which in turn leads to deeper processes of reflection and innovation, whilst also creating the conditions for political instability. The conclusion that we argue can be drawn is that human maladaptation is not unnatural; it is an outsized natural process, which becomes pathological.

Double binds

One of the clearest descriptions of human maladaptation is given by Bateson in his codification of the double bind (Bateson et al. 1956). In the double bind, conflicting messages are presented in social systems at different levels, and a prohibition is placed on individuals subject to these messages such that they are prevented from expressing the whole dynamic they are caught in. The first bind is a contradiction between different anticipatory systems; the second bind is a deprivation of energetic resources necessary to free oneself.

Double binds can arise through the construction of ineffective anticipatory system, or in the conflict between the predictions of different anticipatory systems. Since energy is both necessary for the construction of effective anticipation, a loss of energy can result in a process resulting in poorly coordinated action leading to energy depletion which further damages the ability to anticipate. In the classic double bind, conflicting levels of communication result from different orders of selection. For example, physiological mechanisms may result in facial or bodily expressions of unhappiness or irritation, while linguistic mechanisms may select communication which contradicts the physiological messages. To attempt to interpret a coherent message between these conflicting messages means making the construction of a reliable anticipatory mechanism impossible, leading to a loss of energy in its construction. As energy is lost, the chances of reconstructing an anticipatory system recede in the relationship.

The pathologies of the double bind become worse if the anticipatory system of the person responsible for conflicting messages sees the effect of the double-bind on the other as an opportunity to increase their own energetic reward in an immediate way. In other words, manipulating double binds can become a strategy for acquiring energy at the expense of others, with deception as a principal mechanism at the heart of the process.

It is not just in abusive relationships that this kind of deception operates. Capitalist organisation derives from similar forms of deception. From the origins of the banking system through to trade in derivatives, the dynamics of mixed messages and deception serves to distribute energy to particular actors in the system, who are capable of creating effective anticipatory mechanisms, and thus increase their hold on others in their environment. The sources of corporate profit through products, foodstuffs, etc creates particular forms of deception in the form of offers for energetic reward at the expense of health, get rich quick schemes and so on. This creates higher-order complexities as societies then seek to dig themselves out of epidemics of obesity, poverty, homelessness and collapses in mental health.

At the heart of this process is language. Machiavelli believed that language, like every other human tool, serves the interest of some to the detriment of others. When advising the Prince, he says:

Every one sees what you appear to be, few really know what you are, and those few dare not oppose themselves to the opinion of the many, who have the majesty of the state to defend them; and in the actions of all men, and especially of princes, which it is not prudent to challenge, one judges by the result. (Machiavelli 2014)

It is as if physiological principles which operate at a small scale in cellular communication, when scaled-up to human communicative systems, become pathological. The scaling-up occurs because the patterns are common in nature. But the pathologies it introduces at the human scale are new and self-destructive. Tools are central to this evolutionary development. Human technology extends the time-span through which energy dynamics operate, thus amplifying the force of communicative acts, for good and for ill.

Non-Maladaptive Learning

Part 3 addresses the elision between the technologies described in Part 1 with the evolutionary biological mechanisms of Part 2 with specific focus on the organisation of education.

Collective Bounded Rationality in Learning

The education system brings particular challenges in thinking about maladaptation. Schools and Universities serve as bureaucratic concentrators of power and status which, while not to diminish the utility of specialised inquiry, create divisions and artificial scarcity of knowledge (Illich 1995). Conceiving an education system in a world of tool-supported meta-rationality is to rethink the management of learning as a process of distributing power, status and expertise by predicting where the epistemological contradictions that lead to concentrations of power and status are likely to arise, and allocating resources to address those difficulties. In doing this, a meta-rational system provides the resources to encourage creativity and individual self-discovery as a means of negotiating those activities which are most beset with uncertainty and the potential for conflict.

Meta-rational tools are not specifically educational technology; they represent technologies for a different kind of management. This is because human maladaptation manifests in decisions. Decisions about curricula, qualifications, or the worth of individuals dominate educations dynamics, just as decisions about how to address global warming, or poverty and injustice dominate politics. And these domains of decision are connected. As Simon argued, all decisions are made within constraints which can be divided into:

a)        the context for decision making;

b)       the individual capabilities for reasoning with information about the context and the problem.

Within a), there are the immediate constraints which include the available information about the situation, its accuracy, the technological resources available, power relations within the decision-making group, information we have about others and the broader political background of the decision situation. Within b) there are the skills and capabilities of individuals, their psychological state, and communication channels for coordinating action. Considering these constraints, rather than see human behaviour as rational behaviour, Simon (1983) suggested that humans are engaged in a similar process of negotiating constraints as we have described in the cell. Humans, according to Simon, are satisficers, operating heuristically within the communication environment to match up their internal cognitive models with the external communicative environment. The problem with human satisficing, as we have argued, is that satisficing involves large-scale deception through language and technology. Williamson (1993) famously labelled this key behavioural condition as opportunism: self-interest seeking with guile. Given this, is there a way of conceiving of the bounded rationality of the collective?

Cohen and March (1972), detailed a concrete example of collective satisficing, describing how the information of the environment in board decision-making is manipulated through language. According to Cohen et al., complex problems are ignored and simpler solutions are sought in decision-making scenarios. The committees deception is to decide action that seeks a short-cut in order to pursue its own interests, usually (in Cohen et al.s view) in the form of a discarded technology which is pulled from the garbage can of discarded solutions. The committee, realising its ability to anticipate the will of the people hungry for a decision and answers, deprives the people of sufficient resources to anticipate their environment, thus strengthening its own position, whilst neutralising the efforts to challenge it.

The prominence of deception in Cohen et al.s description of collective decision-making presents a challenging use-case for the use of meta-rational tools. Each actor in the decision-making scenario uses deception as a way of managing the ambiguity of their own environment. The central goal of codifying uncertainty is the identification of what Howard calls stand-offs. These are paradoxical situations where uncertainty is greatest, where the desires and goals of specific groups are in conflict. So what if the manifest uncertainties of the decision situation could be codified, and this used to focus the discussion on those issues where the uncertainties are greatest? This creates the situation where:

           groups likely to be involved in specific stand-offs can be identified (for example, in the case of obesity, consumers, food producers, health workers);

           resources can be allocated to deal with specific stand-offs between specific groups;

Seen in this way, collective bounded rationality is a way organising multiple entities through preserving information within the whole system. This is a good description of what happens in nature with multi-cellular organisms where the organic and individual heuristics of the cell, work together with the heuristics of the organism as a whole. This holism is achieved because the energy resources of organisation and transformation are effectively allocated, enabling each part of the organism to develop its niche in harmony with each other.

To think collectively is to think beyond the bounded rationality of the individual, and instead to consider the dynamics of the niche within which judgements are formed. Therefore we can say that the key features of meta-rationality are:

1.         a means of anticipating ambiguity in the collective environment;

2.         a means of coordinating collective uncertainty and action to produce a niche for the emergence of effective organisation;

3.         a means of conserving information in the total system.

Educational decision-making provides a good example. Typical problems in most universities worldwide include student retention, student recruitment, student wellbeing, teaching, learning and assessment. It is quite usual to find separate committees focusing on each of these topics. In the process, information is lost at the boundaries between (for example) student retention and student recruitment, or between teaching and learning and assessment. A meta-rational tool pinpoints the elevated uncertainties between teaching and assessment practice and retention (for example), focusing resources on specifically addressing the dilemmas at the boundary, and inviting new creative approaches to resolving the stand-off. At the same time, this approach breaks-down the concentration of power and expertise in each domain and distributes the creative energy more broadly in addressing it.

Physiology and Power

Educations role as the locus of the genesis of power relations and the social status-quo is well-documented (Bourdieu and Passeron 1990; Bernstein 2000; Freire 1996). Ecologically-minded groups may push for sustainability in education, exhorting changes to curricula, but if the essential power-creating role of education isnt addressed, then education will remain an engine of human maladaptation.

Politics arises at points of disagreement between different views of the world. Within a meta-rational tool, political flashpoints or stand-offs are represented by regions of high uncertainty. One of the reasons why political problems can become unmanageable is because concentrations of energy are formed which place individuals at a disadvantage in being able to see the dynamics they are in. Exacerbated double binds follow. In Howards original work on meta-rationality, these double-binds (or paradoxes of rationality) can be inspected for the assumptions and missing information relating to different possible strategies and meta-strategies for action. By analysing these assumptions, equilibrium points among the possible meta-strategies between agents in a stand-off or conflict can be identified and the conflict de-escalated (as was done in the Bosnian war with the use of Howards work (1999)).

The goal of a meta-rational tool is to assist in the formation of effective and reliable anticipatory systems: it may be regarded as the use of AI in doing some of the heavy-lifting of Howards systems analysis. Stand-offs and conflict are related to the asymmetry of energy distribution resulting directly from languages throwing-away of information. The energy expended by individuals subject to the double binds in seeking information about their environment is not equal to the energy gained from acquiring new information that feeds the construction of an anticipatory system. Examples of this can be found in social behaviours of continual searching and browsing of social media where the energy expended in searching is not equal to the energy gained from what is discovered. Consequently the ability to search purposefully is impaired because there isnt enough energy to reformulate the anticipatory system leaving the individual expending more energy in searching aimlessly.

In the light of the preceding explanation for energy distribution and natural process, it is easy for a centre of power to put the population in a double-bind because it becomes an energy glut, and this is a natural, but ultimately destructive, process. In order to prevent this happening, a meta-level understanding of the pathology that evolution and language have bequeathed us is necessary - along with new tools that will help us to think against natural tendencies.

Experts and Education

In a university, one expects to find experts who possess knowledge, skill, experience and social connections in a particular domain. An expert is a person who has absorbed energy from the environment and utilised it to create a sophisticated anticipatory system that operates within a particular domain. Information, money and status alongside intersubjective engagements and associated epigenetic signals from a variety of different environments all form part of this absorbed environment. The experts anticipatory system helps to select appropriate utterances in domains that produce powerful interventions. Like a vast natural resource, these utterances convey some of the energy inherent in the construction of the anticipatory system. Most experts do not set out to deceive, but deception is a necessary feature of their interaction because language discards large amounts of information and few experts wish to expose their uncertainty publicly.

In this sense, an expert is an energy glut: a concentration of energy which, whether intentionally or not, is likely to be disposed towards subtle forms of deception in order to maintain their own resources and identity. Illich criticised this dynamic as disabling of the rest of the population (Illich 2005) as when people had problems, experts had solutions and scientists measured imponderables such as abilites and needs . As he points out, the ecological effect of this is to reinforce a scarcity of expert understanding and the establishment of double-binds with the rest of the population who become deprived of the energy to think for themselves. This scarcity has powerful social effects. For example:

           It deceives through marketing the environments in which it appears that expertise is gained, such as an educational institution.

           It reinforces the scarcity of institutional and personal conditions for the gaining of knowledge - for example, contact with famous names

           It creates social division as a knock-on effect of the scarcity of social settings for the acquisition of expertise

           It reinforces capital accumulation as a means of gaining access to educational settings

           Media amplifies the deception, meaning that international coverage will reinforce the scarcity of the expert creating social and national division

           It risks creating a backlash of those positioned disadvantageously in society to distrust wisdom, scholarship and expertise

What would a university look like if it didnt have experts? How might human beings not organise themselves as energy gluts? While expertise can be distributed with online videos and MOOCs, these do nothing to address the problem of the energy glut within the expert: indeed, they are means of amplifying the status of the expert.

To think how expertise might truly be distributed is to consider the analysis, condification and distribution of the uncertainties in the experts judgement. While earlier attempts to distribute expertise through expert systems codified decision-trees for access by lay-people, this threw away the uncertainties involved in making decisions. Instead it is possible to map the uncertainties of an experts judgement through identifying comparative orders of understanding of their knowledge domain. An art expert, for example, will reveal the relative difficulties faced in distinguishing the brush-stokes of Michaelangelo from Raphael, or distinguishing Picasso from Brach.

We argue that this is possible by recognizing that perception is a process of making distinctions between phenomena, wherein those distinctions are ordered in consciousness, and that within those distinctions are variations in uncertainty. As such, expertise relies on an anticipatory system that is a fractal comprising patterns of distinctions at different levels. An ordered set of distinctions carries more information than that contained in the mapping of a phenomenon to a label (such as we described with current AI). In a relational mapping, there are a range of transformations differing in the quantity of energy required to effect a transformation in the mapping. For example, an additional phenomenon that can be easily mapped to existing expectations is clearly different from a paradigm shift which may completely change the mapping of the entire system. This is a greater demand of energy on the system, which is absorbed from the environment and converted into the mental transformation of perception.

Understanding the structural transformations of consciousness in terms of energy expenditure invites the possibility that richer ways of capturing knowledge and understanding might be created which distribute the energy of cognition so that the energy of individual expertise may be made abundant rather than scarce. This may allow for the contribution of individual uncertainty from all levels of society into richer method of social coordination.

Conclusion: Meta-rational Tools and Education

The education system exhibits paradoxes everywhere. School and University teaches competition and deception as much as proponents of sustainability might wish it taught cooperation, empathy and care. There are stand-offs between subjects, technologies, institutions, educational assessments, technology corporations, personal ambitions, teachers and students, and governments. While institutions seek to uphold traditional structures in the face of changing technologies, stand-off positions only become more entrenched, exacerbating existent problems in global and local decision-making for a sustainable future. We have argued that the underlying reasons for these stand-offs in education, as well as the deeper pathologies in society, lie in cellular evolution.

We have made the case for connecting work on meta-rationality for collective decision-making with practical technologies of Artificial Intelligence as tools for meta-rationality. In answer to the original question as to why humans appear to behave so destructively, we have made the case that physiology itself lies behind behaviour where deception is magnified so as to become destructive. While meta-rationality and approaches to analysing it are neither new nor unproven in systems thinking, behind their methodological approach is the analysis of the locus of uncertainty and paradox. In the past, this has required specialist systems analysis. By using a computational approach which connects with physiology, we have suggested that uncertainties and paradoxes may be codified in ways where a technology can target resources on group decision-making more effectively.

The potential of this approach in education is to equip the management of learning with tools that provide greater flexibility in the adaptiveness of educational opportunities to the social environment and to individual learners. Since both individual learners and teachers, as biological systems, and the environment, as a threatened domain, operate on the same biological principles, a biologically-informed technology based around uncertainty presents an opportunity to reconcile our human technology with our biological nature. While technology and language have amplified biological mechanisms of deception, language gives us the capacity to discuss it, while technology gives us the potential to overcome it.
